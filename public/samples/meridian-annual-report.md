# Meridian Cycles Ltd. — Annual Report FY 2025

## Letter from the CEO

Dear shareholders, FY 2025 was the year Meridian stopped being a bicycle
company that experiments with cargo, and became a mobility company with a
bicycle heritage. Revenue grew 18% to ₹482 crore, our cargo segment grew
41%, and we shipped our one-millionth cycle in November — a red Meridian
Swift, bought by a schoolteacher in Vijayawada, whom we flew to our Pune
factory to ring the assembly bell. We also made mistakes this year, which
this report does not hide. — Priya Raghunathan, CEO

## Company overview

Meridian designs and manufactures city bicycles, electric cargo trikes,
and spare parts. Founded in Coimbatore in 1987 as a spoke supplier, the
company today operates two factories (Coimbatore and Pune), 240 dealer
partners, and a direct-to-consumer channel launched in 2023.

## Segment performance: City bicycles

Our core segment grew a modest 6% to ₹291 crore. The Swift and Swift Lite
remain the volume leaders. Average selling price rose 4% after we
discontinued the entry-level Sparrow line, a decision that cost us an
estimated 30,000 unit sales but improved segment margin by 1.9 points.

## Segment performance: Cargo

Cargo is our growth engine: revenue of ₹127 crore, up 41% year on year.
The three-wheeled Mule electric trike now accounts for 71% of segment
revenue, driven by delivery-fleet orders from two national grocery
platforms. Fleet customers now represent 58% of cargo revenue — a
concentration risk we discuss candidly in the Risks section.

## Segment performance: Spares & service

Spares grew 12% to ₹64 crore. Service subscriptions ("Meridian Care")
crossed 100,000 active members. Attach rate at point of sale reached 27%,
up from 19%.

## Regional performance

South India remains our stronghold at 52% of revenue. The North grew
fastest (+31%), led by Delhi NCR fleet deployments. Exports — Sri Lanka,
Nepal, and a pilot in Kenya — contributed ₹18 crore, our first year above
the ₹15 crore threshold that triggers a dedicated export division under
our internal charter.

## Sustainability

The Pune factory now runs on 62% solar power (target: 80% by FY 2027).
We eliminated single-use plastics from packaging in March. Our
take-back-and-refurbish program collected 8,400 old cycles, of which
6,100 were refurbished and donated to school mobility programs through
the Meridian Foundation.

## People

Headcount grew from 1,840 to 2,015. Voluntary attrition fell to 9%, the
lowest in company history, which we attribute to the four-day workweek
pilot in the Coimbatore plant — retained permanently after productivity
rose 3% during the trial. Women now hold 31% of supervisory roles, up
from 24%.

## Risks and what worries us

Three risks keep management up at night. First, fleet concentration: two
customers are 58% of cargo revenue; losing either would erase most of the
segment's growth. Second, lithium cell supply: 87% of our cells come from
a single Taiwanese supplier; we have signed a second-source agreement in
Gujarat that begins deliveries in Q3 FY 2026. Third, regulatory: proposed
e-trike classification rules could require licensing that dampens fleet
demand. We have submitted comments to the ministry and planned a
pedal-assist variant as a hedge.

## The mistake we made this year

We over-forecast festive-season demand and built 22,000 units of
inventory that took five months to clear at reduced margin, costing
approximately ₹9 crore in profit. The planning process now includes a
dealer pre-order signal and a hard cap on speculative builds.

## Outlook for FY 2026

We guide to revenue of ₹560–580 crore, cargo crossing ₹180 crore, and
the launch of the Mule Mini, a narrow-lane variant designed for old-city
logistics. Capital expenditure of ₹45 crore is planned, of which ₹30
crore is for the Pune paint shop expansion.

## Dividend

The board recommends a dividend of ₹6 per share (FY 2024: ₹4.50),
a payout ratio of 24%.

## Board of directors

The board comprises nine members, four independent. Ms. Anjali Verma
joined as independent director, bringing supply-chain expertise from her
tenure at a national logistics firm. The board met eleven times; average
attendance was 92%.

## Auditor's note

The statutory auditors issued an unqualified opinion. One key audit
matter: revenue recognition on fleet contracts with volume rebates, which
the auditors tested by recalculating rebate provisions across all
contracts above ₹1 crore.

## Quarterly performance

Q1 was our weakest quarter (₹98 crore) due to the inventory overhang
described earlier. Q2 recovered to ₹118 crore on fleet deliveries. Q3,
containing the festive season, remains the largest at ₹146 crore despite
the forecasting miss. Q4 closed at ₹120 crore with the strongest margin
of the year (35.1% gross) as the discounted inventory cleared out.

## Product development

R&D expense rose to ₹19 crore (3.9% of revenue). The year's major
program was the Mule Mini: 14 months from concept to production-ready,
including 40,000 km of loaded road testing across Chennai's old town,
Varanasi's ghavat lanes, and Shillong's gradients. Two programs were
cancelled: a carbon-frame road bike (unit economics never closed) and a
subscription swap-battery scheme (regulatory uncertainty). We consider
disciplined cancellation a feature of our process, not a failure of it.

## Manufacturing and supply chain

The Coimbatore plant produced 141,000 units at 91% capacity utilization;
Pune produced 85,000 units at 76%. Supplier count was reduced from 214
to 168 as part of the quality consolidation program; incoming defect
rates fell 22%. Steel and aluminium prices were stable, but lithium cells
rose 9% — partially hedged by the annual contract signed in April. The
Gujarat second-source cell agreement covers up to 30% of projected FY27
cell demand.

## Digital and direct-to-consumer

The D2C channel reached ₹41 crore (8.5% of revenue) in its second year.
The Meridian app crossed 400,000 installs; connected-bike features
(trip logging, theft alerts) are attached to 64% of premium-model sales.
Website-to-dealer referrals — where a customer configures online and
collects at a dealer — grew 3x and now represent our highest-conversion
funnel, which is why FY26 investment favours dealer-integration tooling
over pure e-commerce.

## Corporate social responsibility

CSR spend was ₹1.4 crore, meeting the statutory 2% requirement. Flagship
programs: cycle-to-school grants (6,100 refurbished cycles donated),
mechanic apprenticeships for 240 rural youth (78% job placement), and
road-safety workshops reaching 45,000 schoolchildren. The board's CSR
committee met four times and conducted two field audits.

## Shareholding pattern

Promoter holding stands at 51.2% (unchanged). Institutional investors
hold 22.4%, up from 18.9%, following two domestic mutual fund entries.
Retail shareholders number 48,000, our highest ever. No shares are
pledged. The employee stock option pool has 3.1% outstanding, with the
FY25 grant covering 380 employees down to the supervisor level for the
first time.

## Awards and recognition

Meridian received the National Design Award for the Mule cargo platform,
was named Supplier of the Year by our largest grocery fleet customer,
and entered the "Great Place to Work" list at rank 74. The Pune plant's
solar installation won the state renewable-energy award for industrial
retrofits.

## Management discussion: competition

Competitive intensity increased. Two e-mobility startups launched cargo
trikes at prices 15–20% below the Mule; both currently lose money per
unit, a strategy we cannot and will not match. Our response is service
coverage — 240 dealer workshops against their combined 31 service
points — and total-cost-of-ownership selling to fleet buyers, where our
38% lower three-year maintenance cost decides tenders. We lost two
municipal tenders on sticker price this year and won five fleet renewals
on TCO; we are content with that trade."
