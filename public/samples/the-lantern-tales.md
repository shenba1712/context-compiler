# The Lantern Tales

*Twelve small stories to read by whatever light you have.*

## Tale One: The Moth Who Argued with the Flame

A moth spent every evening arguing with a candle flame. "You are wasteful,"
said the moth. "You burn the whole night whether anyone reads by you or not."
The flame flickered and replied, "And you spend your one life circling what
you cannot hold." They argued until the wax ran out, and in the sudden dark
the moth discovered the moon — enormous, patient, and impossible to argue
with. From that night on the moth flew higher, and the house was quieter,
and the candles lasted twice as long.

## Tale Two: The Tortoise and the River

A tortoise wished to cross the Kaveri where it ran fastest. The river
laughed at her: "You are too slow. I will carry you where I please." The
tortoise said nothing. She walked upstream for three days, to the place
where the river was born between two stones and was only a finger wide, and
stepped across it in one step. "Speed," she told the minnows, "is knowing
where the crossing is short."

## Tale Three: The Weaver of Small Hours

In Madurai lived a weaver who could not sleep, so she wove between midnight
and dawn. Into every sari she wove one thread of grey — "the hour nobody
wants," she called it. Years later, buyers noticed her saris never faded.
The dye-masters begged for her secret and she told them plainly: "Cloth
remembers the patience it was made with." Nobody believed her, which suited
her fine, because it kept the small hours quiet.

## Tale Four: The Fox Who Sold Winter

A fox arrived in a village that had never seen snow and announced he had
winter for sale. His price was three promises: that the baker would save
his burnt loaves for the birds, that the well would never be locked, and
that no one would cut the old banyan for firewood. The villagers agreed,
laughing at the mad fox. That December, snow fell for the first time in
memory. It melted by noon — but the promises, being promises, did not.

## Tale Five: The Clockmaker's Sparrow

A clockmaker trained a sparrow to wind the tower clock — exactly forty
turns of the key, every morning, in exchange for a spoon of millet. When
the clockmaker died, the town forgot the clock entirely, but the sparrow
did not. For eleven more years the tower kept perfect time, and the town
praised the dead man's engineering. Only the miller's daughter, who rose
before dawn, knew the truth, and she kept the millet coming.

## Tale Six: The Girl Who Bottled Echoes

Ilsa collected echoes in green glass bottles — her grandmother's laugh,
rain on the tin roof, the last bell of her school before it closed. She
labelled each cork in pencil. When the flood took the village's photographs,
people came to her door. She charged nothing, but kept one rule: you had to
listen to your echo twice. "Once to grieve," she said, "and once to be
glad."

## Tale Seven: The Bear Who Kept the Inn

The inn at the mountain pass was kept by a bear who had grown tired of
being feared. He learned to make soup, kept the fire high, and never asked
travellers where they were going. His only house rule hung above the door:
"Leave your knives with your boots." One winter a wolf-pack came as guests
and left as regulars. The soup, they agreed, was worth the disarming.

## Tale Eight: The Cartographer of Puddles

After every monsoon, old Devan mapped the puddles of the town — which ones
held the sky longest, which reflected the temple gopuram, which one the
schoolchildren could not resist. "Rivers get atlases," he said. "Puddles
get me." When the town paved its lanes, his maps became the only record
that the sky had once been kept, briefly and in pieces, on the ground.

## Tale Nine: The Thief Who Stole Tuesday

A thief once stole a whole Tuesday — slipped it out from between Monday
and Wednesday while the town slept. He unrolled it in his cellar and found
it full of ordinary things: a dentist's appointment, a half-watered garden,
an apology someone had planned to make. The apology troubled him. He
returned the day by dawn, kept nothing, and made the apology himself, on a
Thursday, which everyone agreed was somehow braver.

## Tale Ten: The Lighthouse That Blinked Twice

Every lighthouse on the coast blinked once. The keeper at Point Elba set
hers to blink twice — one flash for "rocks here," she said, and one for
"you are not alone out there." Sailors mocked the sentiment and steered by
it anyway. Forty years later, when the light was automated, the engineers
kept the double blink. No one could prove it saved ships. No one dared
remove it either.

## Tale Eleven: The Baker of Borrowed Salt

Amma Baker never bought salt. She borrowed it, a pinch at a time, from
every house on the street, and repaid it in bread. "Salt carries the hand
it came from," she insisted. Her loaves tasted of the whole neighbourhood —
of the teacher's tin, the tailor's jar, the widow's chipped cup. When she
died, the street discovered no one knew the recipe, because the recipe was
everyone.

## Tale Twelve: The Old Dog and the Comet

An old dog watched a comet cross the sky and decided, privately, that it
was a stick. He waited nine years for it to land, checking the horizon
each night after dinner. It never landed, but the waiting kept him walking
to the hilltop long past the age dogs stop climbing hills. The vet called
it a miracle of health. The dog called it fetch, played slowly.

## Tale Thirteen: The Ferryman's Fee

The ferryman at Kollur crossing never took coins. His fee was a story —
one per passenger, true or invented, he didn't care, so long as it was
theirs. Merchants grumbled and paid in gossip; children paid in dragons.
When he retired, he had no money and no house, so the village built him
one, because by then every family's best story had his boat in it
somewhere, and you look after the library you keep your memories in.

## Tale Fourteen: The Queen Who Counted Backwards

Queen Ravenna aged like everyone else, but counted her birthdays
backwards, starting from one hundred. "I am sixty-two," she announced,
meaning she had thirty-eight years left, "and I will not spend one of the
remaining on flattery." Her court found this morbid until they noticed
the effect: she heard petitions faster, forgave debts sooner, and planted
trees whose shade she would never sit in. She died at what she called
eleven. The trees are enormous now.

## Tale Fifteen: The Boy Made of Questions

Tomas asked so many questions that his village sent him to the mountain
hermit, hoping the old man would exhaust him. They asked each other
questions for three days. On the fourth, the hermit came down the
mountain alone and opened a school. "He asked me the one I couldn't
answer," the hermit admitted — "'Why do you keep your wisdom where no
one can trip over it?'" Tomas stayed up there for years, answering
letters, one question at a time.

## Tale Sixteen: The Umbrella Lender

On the rainiest street in Mawsynram stood a stand of forty-one umbrellas
and a sign: TAKE ONE, RETURN ANY. Not "return it" — return *any*. Over
the years the umbrellas changed colour, size, and country of origin, as
travellers swapped what they'd taken in other cities for what they took
here. The stand's keeper said she was not lending umbrellas at all. "I am
lending the habit of returning things. The umbrellas are just how it
travels."

## Tale Seventeen: The Painter of Closed Doors

Mireille painted only doors — always closed ones. Critics called the work
repetitive until her retrospective, when all two hundred paintings hung
in one hall, and visitors realized every knob was polished bright by use
and every doormat was worn. "Closed doesn't mean locked," said the little
card at the entrance. People walked out of that show and knocked on doors
they had walked past for decades. Two weddings and one reconciled family
are documented results.

## Tale Eighteen: The Night Market of Lost Things

Behind the railway yard, on new-moon nights only, a market sold what the
world had misplaced: single earrings, childhood nicknames, the knack of
whistling. You could not buy back your own losses — house rule — only
someone else's, to return to them. Trade was brisk and no money changed
hands. The stallkeepers took payment in one currency: you had to tell
them what losing it had taught you.

## Tale Nineteen: The Lion Who Learned to Garden

A retired circus lion was given a cottage and, out of boredom, a packet
of tomato seeds. He was terrible at it — paws are not made for
seedlings — but persistent, and by the third summer his garden fed half
the lane. Children who had been warned about him came for cuttings. "The
trick," he told them, tasting a tomato, "is the same as the circus. You
cannot rush a living thing. You can only show up every day until it
trusts you."

## Tale Twenty: The Librarian and the Unwritten Book

Every library has one shelf that stays empty, and Mrs. Okonkwo guarded
hers fiercely. "It is for the books not written yet," she said, dusting
the bare wood weekly. Regulars rolled their eyes for years — until a
quiet girl asked if the shelf could hold *her* book someday, and Mrs.
Okonkwo replied, "It has been waiting for it." The girl grew up, wrote
it, and the shelf now holds one book and a card: ONE DOWN.

## Tale Twenty-One: The Bridge That Charged Kindness

The toll bridge at Harrow Vale accepted no silver. To cross, you owed one
kindness on the far side, to a stranger, before sunset — the bridge
keeper trusted you, which was the unnerving part. Cheats simply found
the far village cold to them forever after; word crossed the bridge
faster than travellers did. Merchants budgeted for it in their ledgers,
a line item between rope and salt: "bridge kindness, one, various."

## Tale Twenty-Two: The Clock That Ran on Apologies

The courthouse clock of Dunmore stopped whenever the town held a grudge
too long — nobody knew why, and the horologists gave up. The mayor
learned to read it like weather: a stopped clock meant two neighbours
needed a nudge. After the great feud of the fishing quotas, it stayed
stopped for a month, and started again — witnesses swear — at the exact
moment old Brennan shook hands with the harbourmaster. The town keeps
apologizing promptly now. Punctuality, they call it.

## Tale Twenty-Three: The Girl Who Traded Her Shadow

A travelling magician offered Lina gold for her shadow, and she agreed —
who uses a shadow? But without it, bakers forgot her order, dogs walked
through her heels, and her grandmother's stories skipped her name. A
person, it turns out, is partly the space they occupy. She tracked the
magician for a year and won it back not with gold but with the one thing
he had never owned: she guessed, correctly, that he had sold his own
shadow first, and offered him her place at a family table instead.

## Tale Twenty-Four: The Last Lamplighter

When the city electrified, it kept one gas lamp and one lamplighter, out
of sentiment, on Wren Lane. Tomas — the same Tomas, much older — climbed
the ladder every dusk for thirty years, and a crowd slowly became a
custom: you brought your unanswerable question to the lighting of the
lamp and let it go with the flame. The city thinks it is preserving
heritage. Wren Lane knows better. Some lights are kept for what people
leave beside them.
